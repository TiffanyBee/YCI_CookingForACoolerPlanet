Foreward

We understand the struggle of learning to cook and eating healthy. With the internet, sure there are many places to find information, but it can be scattered and overwhelming. In your busy life, there are already plenty of things flooding your brain, but a good meal shouldn’t be one of them.

A fantasy utopia exists in the minds of those who dream that every bite helps the body and heals the planet. Eating green is the promise guaranteed to bridge the two worlds. Eating green is a diet rich in plant based foods such as roots, vegetables, fruits, and grains that prevents disease and supports a superb lifestyle supplying the nutrients a healthy human needs. This method is equally beneficial for the other partner in the relationship with humans: the Earth. Eating locally grown food, organic produce, or greens, significantly lowers the greenhouse gasses which coats our atmosphere like a blanket. A common misconception has plagued the conversation surrounding carbon emissions, as they are perceived as all harmful, when they are necessary in order for humans to survive. Greenhouse gasses act as insulators to Earth’s atmosphere, as they reflect the warmth given from the sun when it rises from the ground and attempts to escape.

However, the balance has been disrupted with the overproduction of carbon emissions which leads to the insulation of too much heat, suffocating the earth and preventing heat from escaping. The rise in temperature has led and will continue to follow through a chain reaction of change throughout the ecosystem that all affect one another. The ecosystem is all connected, even in unforeseen ways. When the carbon emissions increase, the temperature follows, causing ice caps to melt which raises sea levels and leads to flooding and other natural disasters, which is an economic and emotional catastrophe. A marine biologist might view this change as a tragedy for marine life, as a rise in sea temperature would lead to mass death to stretch across corals as bleaching events occur. 25% of marine life relies on coral reefs, so once coral dies, the animals that rely on them are in danger as a result.

Although this ecosystem shows how danger and harm can spread through its connective anatomy, positive events can ripple waves to the same extent. For example, eating green has an effect on not only one part of the environment, but many as that lifestyle carries through many corners of the ecosystem.

As of now, meat is generally a staple in American dishes. An average U.S. resident consumes 224.6 pounds of meat per year. Although meat is a delicious main course ingredient, not only is an excess amount dire for your health, it also has detrimental damage on the planet. Meats, and animal-based foods, are associated with the highest greenhouse gas emissions because of their processes of obtaining the food. With trees being cut down to make a grassland suitable for meat production and livestock releasing methane into the ecosystem, an unhealthy amount of greenhouse gasses continue to form in our atmosphere. 1 kilogram of beef, a meal for a party of 3 or 4 people, results in 70.6 kilograms of greenhouse gasses being released.

But don’t panic! While it's true that meat consumption has negative impacts on our environment, it isn’t necessary to discard it from your diet. We’re not demanding you to become fully vegan, or even vegetarian, in fact studies have shown that both mediterranean and vegan diets have significantly less of a carbon footprint compared to the average American. So, As you flip through the pages of this guide, you will find a plethora of Mediterranean and vegetarian recipes. Ranging from a filling dinner to a quick snack, we are assured you will find the perfect meal for your mood.

Methods of Cooking

It is very important to carefully select the method of cooking you will use. Kitchen appliances are the elements that actually use energy, and maybe even emit gasses, so the one you choose will greatly affect how climate friendly your cooking session will go. In the following section we will rank the most commonly used kitchen appliances so you can make a sustainable decision!

  1. Microwave

    Microwaves are great choices since they use little energy for a short amount of time. They are popular for snacks, but not attractive for making meals.

  2. Toaster

    Toasters are quick and easy ways to heat food, but are restricted to a flat-shaped dish.

  3. Air Fryer

    The air fryer has gained popularity recently as a novel kitchen appliance finding its way on the counter of millions of Americans. It is small and uses convection, or hot flowing air to heat food.

  4. Stovetop

    Stoves the are the standard method of cooking, but gas and stovetop are both different in the way that they impact the environment.

  5. Oven

    Ovens are large and standard for pies, cookies, cakes, and other baked dishes, but also lose a lot of heat when the door is opened. Additionally, energy is wasted when preheat time increases.

Using Energy Wisely

Preheating the stove or oven is often listed in recipes, but it also harms the environment as energy is used before the food touches the heat. INstead, for most recipes, reduce the preheat time by either popping the food in the oven or on the stove before it reaches the desired heat or turning on the heat with the food on top.

How Much Use?

The more the appliance is used, the more energy is used. In the case o gas-based appliances, natural gases are burned to create CO2 as a by product. The CO2 molecule is a greenhouse gas and reflects warmth back to earth, instead of allowing it to escape the atmospher. For this reason, avoiding thesee gas-based appliances is encouraged.

Choosing Recipes

Everyone knows we all have to eat but did you know that what you eat and how you prepare it can make an impact on climate change? There are a few simple rules you can follow that will help you decide if a recipe is climate friendly. Here are some of the things you should consider when deciding what meals you are going to prepare.

Consider what ingredients are in a recipe

A general rule is that a recipe that contains mainly ingredients that are plant-based are the most climate-friendly. All animals, before they become an ingredient in your recipe, first must consume plants or animals that eat plants. This means that more energy is used in producing animal ingredients.

Consider What kinds of animal ingredients are in the recipe

Fish that are sustainably caught can be a very climate-friendly ingredient. Because less resources go into raising chickens and turkey, they are better ingredients than beef or lamb. (Actually, substituting turkey for beef in recipes can reduce the amount of greenhouse gasses produced by 5 times!)

In general, dairy products produce more greenhouse gasses than plant-based substances such as margarine and plant oils.

Consider where the ingredients are from

Obviously, those ingredients that come from local sources are better choices that must travel a distance. Local produce, of course, is much more readily available in the warmer months in temperate zones. In the winter, choosing frozen or canned vegetables is better than buying vegetables that must be flown a great distance to reach your grocery store.

Making seasonal recipes is a good rule to follow. For example, strawberries and asparagus are readily available from local sources only in the spring.

Avoiding Food Waste

When you waste food, you waste all the energy and work put into harvesting that same food. Therefore you should make a great effort to reduce food waste in order to reduce the energy being wasted and the food going into the landfills. Luckily, there are easy ways to avoid unnecessary waste.

Check the pantry or fridge before going shopping

It is easy to forget what we already have at our house, and buy food we don’t need. But this mistake can lead to unused food going bad. So not only are you wasting the energy put into making this food, you are wasting the money you spent on the food.

Properly store products in the refrigerator

Putting food in the correct area of the fridge allows your food to last longer. The top shelf should consist of ready to eat foods such as leftovers, meals, and drinks. The middle shelf is reserved for dairy products because the temperature is more consistent. The bottom shelf is for raw meats because it is colder. Refrigerable condiments, such as ketchup, hot sauce, or mustards, should be on the door of the fridge.

Purchase imperfect produce

If food is bad for the consumer, groceries wouldn’t sell them, so don’t be afraid of fruit that looks a little odd. Buying “ugly” fruit reduces wasted energy, supports local farmers, and might save you a quick buck. So try it out!
